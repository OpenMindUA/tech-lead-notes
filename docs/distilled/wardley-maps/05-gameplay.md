# Wardley Maps — gameplay

**TL;DR:** **Gameplay** = the deliberate moves you make on the map to advance your purpose. Unlike doctrine (universal), gameplay is **context-specific** — what works here may fail there. Wardley catalogs ~60 plays grouped by intent: shaping the landscape, accelerating/slowing evolution, defending position, exploiting an opponent, ecosystem moves.

## Categories of plays

### A. User & landscape shaping
- **Creation of new markets** — anchor on an unmet need, build the value chain.
- **Education** — make users aware of a need they didn't know they had.
- **Bundling / unbundling** — change what the user buys together.

### B. Accelerating / slowing evolution
- **Open-source play** (canonical Wardley move) — open-source a component to *push it down* the evolution axis (toward commodity), removing your competitor's margin in that layer while you sell the layer above.
- **Standards game** — drive a public standard so the component commoditizes faster.
- **FUD** — slow a competitor's component evolution by spreading uncertainty.
- **Patent / IP** — slow evolution to defend margins.

### C. Defensive plays
- **Last-man-standing** — in a dying market, be the last incumbent (small but profitable).
- **Pig in a poke** — sell a declining asset before its decline becomes obvious.
- **Differentiation** — add features at product stage to delay commoditization (often only buys time).

### D. Attacking plays
- **Tower & moat** — own a commodity layer (the moat), force everyone above (the tower) to depend on it.
- **Sweat & dump** — extract maximum value from a declining product line, then divest.
- **Embrace and extend** — adopt a competitor's product, extend with proprietary, lock the install base.

### E. Ecosystem plays
- **Two-factor markets** — build a platform where one side's value grows the other's.
- **ILC (Innovate-Leverage-Commoditize)** — let an ecosystem innovate on top of your platform; observe what works; commoditize the winners.
- **Pricing leverage** — adjust pricing of one component to shift demand on another.

### F. Misc
- **Misdirection** — publicly emphasize one move while doing another.
- **Fool's mate** — exploit an opponent's predictable doctrine failure.

(Wardley's full list is much longer; see [Ch.11 — A smorgasbord of the slightly useful](../../sources/wardley-maps/11-a-smorgasbord-of-the-slightly-useful.md).)

## How to choose a play

1. **Map first** (cards 01-02).
2. **Annotate climate** (card 04). Identify which components are about to move.
3. **Check doctrine** (card 03). If doctrine is broken, fix that — gameplay won't save you.
4. **Pick a play** that matches the climate-annotated map *and* your purpose. A play is "right" only relative to a specific map.
5. **Predict the response.** Opponents have maps too (or should). Their counter-play is predictable from their position.

## The open-source play in detail (worked example)

Setup:
- Component X is at product stage. Competitor C has built a profitable proprietary X.
- We sell layer Y *on top of* X. Our margins on Y are squeezed by C's pricing power on X.

Move:
- Open-source a high-quality alternative to X.
- Push community/standards to adopt it.
- X commoditizes faster than it would naturally.
- C's margin on X collapses. Our cost-of-goods for layer Y drops. We capture more value on Y.

Why it works: we used **climate** ("everything evolves") + **gameplay** ("accelerate") to redirect industry economics in our favour.

## When to use this card

- After mapping + climatic annotation, deciding *what to do*.
- Evaluating a competitor's recent move — which play are they running? (the "what game are they playing?" question).
- Designing a portfolio strategy — which plays per business line?

## Anti-patterns

- **Gameplay before doctrine.** See card 03; the most common failure.
- **Gameplay without a map.** "Open-source the SDK!" — at which evolution stage? against whom? for what user need? Without a map, every play is a guess.
- **One play, all situations.** Most "best practice strategy advice" is one play universalized — works in the original context, fails elsewhere.
- **Ignoring opponent counter-plays.** Maps are public knowledge in your industry. Assume opponents see what you see and plan their move.
- **Playing the wrong stage.** Defensive plays at genesis stage = waste. Offensive plays without commodity components below = no leverage.
