# Wardley Maps — doctrine

**TL;DR:** Doctrine = the **universal practices** that apply to *any* organization regardless of context. Apply them before context-specific gameplay. Wardley groups doctrine into phases (communication, development, operation, structure, learning, leading) — each subsequent phase depends on the prior ones being honoured.

## Why doctrine before gameplay

- Gameplay (e.g. open-source play, undermining a competitor) is **context-dependent** — works on this map, fails on another.
- Doctrine is **context-free** — true regardless. Skip it and your gameplay rests on sand.
- Most strategic failures aren't bad gameplay; they're failed doctrine: the org never developed situational awareness, used inappropriate methods, or didn't know its users.

## The doctrine phases (selected highlights)

### Phase I — Communication & shared language
- **Focus on user needs.** Without an anchor, no map; without a map, no shared understanding.
- **Use a common language.** Wardley Maps *are* the common language; without one, every team interprets the strategy differently.
- **Know your users** (each user type) — different needs → different anchors → different maps.

### Phase II — Development
- **Use appropriate methods.** Genesis ≠ custom ≠ product ≠ commodity (see card 02). Single methodology across all = waste.
- **Think small (build small teams of size that you can coordinate, ~12 people).** Communication overhead is real.
- **Distribute power and decision-making** to where the information lives.

### Phase III — Operation
- **Be transparent.** Maps shared > maps hidden.
- **Challenge assumptions** — every component placement, every dependency, every climatic pattern claim.
- **Remove duplication and bias.** Same-but-different components built in five orgs = waste; bias in placement = bad strategy.

### Phase IV — Structure
- **Think aptitude *and* attitude** — different roles need different aptitudes (engineering vs design vs strategy) AND different attitudes (pioneer / settler / town planner). See card 06.
- **Design for constant evolution** — re-org isn't a project; it's the steady state.

### Phase V — Learning from your environment
- **A bias to action over study** — maps are not exhaustive analyses; they are decision aids. Move and update.
- **Listen to your ecosystem.** Patterns repeat; learn from what's evolving around you.
- **Use a systematic mechanism for learning** (e.g. doctrine itself).

### Phase VI — Leading
- **Strategy is iterative, not linear.** The plan you wrote yesterday is wrong today.
- **Be the owner.** Strategic responsibility doesn't outsource.

## Practical use

The doctrine list is a **maturity audit checklist**. Score the org on each, find the lowest, fix that first. Don't jump to gameplay if the org doesn't share a common language about its users.

> "Most organizations fail at doctrine, not strategy."

## When to use this card

- First strategy review at a new org / team — doctrine audit reveals more than KPIs.
- After a strategic miss — diagnose: was it bad gameplay, or had doctrine been skipped?
- Onboarding leaders — give them the audit checklist before letting them play moves.

## Anti-patterns

- **Jumping to gameplay.** Reading the "11 gameplays" chapter and trying tactics before fixing doctrine is the most common mistake.
- **Treating doctrine as optional.** "We don't need a common language, our team is small." → six months later, two teams have built the same component twice.
- **Doctrine compliance as performance theatre.** Saying "we focus on user needs" while having no anchored map.
- **Single methodology.** Same SDLC for everything → see card 02 anti-patterns.
