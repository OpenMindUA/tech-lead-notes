# Wardley Maps — climatic patterns

**TL;DR:** **Climate** = the rules that act on every map regardless of who you are or what you build. They aren't your choices; they happen *to* you. Map them before deciding any move. Headline rules: everything evolves, change is the norm, past success creates inertia, and commoditization enables new genesis.

## Selected climatic patterns

### 1. Everything evolves
Under competitive pressure, every component moves left → right (genesis → commodity). Resisting this is fighting physics. Time-frames vary (years to decades) but direction is one-way.

### 2. Change is the norm
The map you have today is not the map you'll have in 3 years. Strategy that assumes a static landscape will be wrong by definition.

### 3. Past success breeds inertia
The more successful you've been with a component in stage III, the harder you'll resist its move to stage IV — *even though refusing the move is what kills you*. Inertia takes many forms:
- Investment in existing assets
- Skills mismatch ("we don't know utility")
- Cultural ("but we *built* this")
- Political (someone's job depends on it)
- Customer/partner ("they expect us to keep doing it the old way")

### 4. Commoditization enables new sources of worth
When component X commoditizes (e.g. compute → AWS), it unlocks an explosion of higher-order **genesis** (e.g. cloud-native startups). The deflation of one layer fuels the inflation of the next. **Spot the wave** — that's where the next gameplay opportunity sits.

### 5. Efficiency does not preclude innovation, it enables it
Cheap, ubiquitous compute didn't kill innovation; it *caused* an innovation explosion. Standardization at one layer → freedom at the layer above.

### 6. No choice over evolution
You can affect *speed* (slow it, accelerate it through gameplay) but not direction. Pretending otherwise is a strategic dead-end.

### 7. Characteristics change with stage
Components don't just move; their *properties* change at each stage:
- Margins: high in product, low in commodity
- Failure tolerance: high in genesis, near-zero in commodity (utility outage = headlines)
- Methods that work: agile/lean/six-sigma/etc. each fit different stages

### 8. Coevolution of practice and activity
When activity X commoditizes, the *practices* around it also evolve. Cloud (commodity activity) drove DevOps (practice) — neither could have happened separately.

### 9. Punctuated equilibrium
Evolution isn't linear. Long stable periods get punctuated by rapid shifts (new paradigm appears, dominant designs converge fast).

## How to use climatic patterns

1. Draw your map.
2. For each component, ask which patterns apply *to that component over the next 1-3 years*.
3. Annotate predicted moves on the map with arrows.
4. Use the annotated map to decide gameplay.

> The map plus its climatic annotation is your **forecast** — not a guarantee, but vastly better than anchorless intuition.

## When to use this card

- Long-range planning (1-5 years) — most strategy fails because climate was ignored.
- Investment decisions on a long-lived component — will it still be product-stage when ROI lands?
- Re-org / re-platforming debates — climatic forecast disambiguates "do nothing" vs "act now".
- Spotting the next wave: where is something *just* commoditizing? Build there.

## Anti-patterns

- **Treating the map as static.** A snapshot, not a forecast. Without arrows, the map is half-useful.
- **Underestimating inertia.** "We'll just shift." No, you won't — your installed base, skills, and political incentives all push back. Plan the *transition*, not the destination.
- **Confusing speed with direction.** You can slow X's evolution to commodity, but if commoditization is happening industry-wide you cannot stop it.
- **Treating each pattern in isolation.** They compose — *everything evolves* + *inertia* + *coevolution* together explain most strategic surprises.
