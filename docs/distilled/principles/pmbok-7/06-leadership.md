# Demonstrate leadership behaviors

**TL;DR:** Leadership is influencing others toward desired outcomes —
a *behavior*, not a job title. Anyone can lead; PMs should both
*be* leaders and *find and empower* hidden leaders.

## Core ideas

- **Leadership ≠ management role.** Anyone who influences people
  toward outcomes is a leader, regardless of position. Some of the
  highest-impact people in an organization hold non-leadership
  titles (e.g. a long-tenured admin who informally coordinates
  everything).
- **Identify and support hidden leaders.** They self-actualize
  through the work; small denials (no raise, no recognition) push
  them out and the loss only becomes visible afterwards.
- **Motivation is contextual.** A monetary bonus motivates only when
  the amount-to-need ratio is high enough. A small cash amount can
  *demotivate*; a symbolic gift (e.g. engraved pen + sincere thanks)
  on the same budget often outperforms it.
- **Charisma is plural.** Charisma forms include knowledge,
  kindness, supportiveness, calm confidence — not only
  movie-style toughness. Find your natural form and develop it.
- **Skill set (per APMBoK):**
    - Communication
    - Conflict management
    - Delegation
    - Influencing
    - Leadership
    - Negotiation
    - Teamwork

## When this applies

- Recognizing and rewarding non-managerial contributors
- Designing a recognition / bonus / appreciation programme
- Choosing how to deliver praise or hard feedback
- Personal development planning for PMs
- Retention conversations (don't lose hidden leaders cheaply)

## Anti-patterns

- Treating "leadership" as synonymous with "manager" or "PM"
- Trying to imitate a single charisma stereotype that fits poorly
- Low-amount cash bonuses that imply the contribution is cheap
- Letting key informal leaders leave over a denied small raise
