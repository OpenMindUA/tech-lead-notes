# Stewardship — *Be a diligent, respectful, and caring steward*

**TL;DR:** A project manager is a facilitator and steward of people
and resources, not a technical decision-maker or a "boss".

## Core ideas

- **Manager ≠ promoted expert.** Becoming a manager is a *career
  change*, not a *promotion*. Managerial skill is independent of
  technical skill; promoting senior experts into PM roles loses a
  good expert and gains a mediocre manager.
- **PM as chief facilitator.** A PM coordinates, removes obstacles,
  resolves conflict, and creates a safe productive environment so
  experts can do their work. Renaming the role to "chief project
  facilitator" can correct culture if "PM" carries baggage.
- **Technical knowledge is optional, possibly counter-productive.**
  Three positions exist; the author favors the strongest: a PM
  *without* technical knowledge is less likely to micromanage. If
  you do have technical expertise, **draw a hard line** between
  technical and managerial decisions and stay out of the former.
- **Stewardship covers people and resources.** Care, integrity,
  trustworthiness, compliance with rules and ethics.
- **Listen to understand, not to respond.** Initiate conversation;
  hunt for problems early.

## When this applies

- Selecting or promoting project managers
- Defining the PM job description and competency framework
- Coaching new PMs out of "technical boss" habits
- Choosing whether to staff a PM with or without domain expertise

## Anti-patterns

- Promoting the most senior technical expert "as a reward"
- A PM who micromanages technical decisions because they "know better"
- PM-as-status-symbol with no service mindset
- Waiting for issues to surface instead of seeking them out
