# The Sprint

**TL;DR:** Fixed-length timebox of **one month or less** that
contains all Scrum work. **Heartbeat of Scrum** — turns ideas into
value. New Sprint starts immediately after the previous one ends.

## Properties

- **Fixed length.** One month max. Consistency matters.
- **No gap.** A new Sprint starts immediately after the last ends.
- **Contains all other events** (Planning, Daily, Review, Retro).
- **Each Sprint is a short project.**

## Rules during a Sprint

- **No changes** that would endanger the Sprint Goal.
- **Quality does not decrease.**
- **Product Backlog is refined** as needed (continuous activity).
- **Scope may be clarified** and renegotiated **with the Product
  Owner** as the team learns more.

## Length trade-off

- **Shorter** Sprints → more learning cycles, lower risk per cycle.
- **Longer** Sprints → Sprint Goal may go invalid, complexity rises,
  risk grows.
- Inspection and adaptation must happen **at least every calendar
  month**.

## Cancellation

- A Sprint can be cancelled **only if the Sprint Goal becomes
  obsolete**.
- **Only the Product Owner** has the authority to cancel.

## Forecasting

- Burn-down, burn-up, cumulative flow are useful but **don't
  replace empiricism**.
- In complex environments, only what has happened informs forward
  decisions.

## When this applies

- Choosing Sprint length (typically 1-, 2-, 3-week)
- Handling mid-Sprint scope-change requests
- Deciding when to cancel a Sprint
- Planning the Scrum cadence calendar

## Anti-patterns

- **Variable-length Sprints** to "fit the work"
- Trading quality for speed inside a Sprint
- Stakeholders changing scope mid-Sprint without negotiating with PO
- Cancelling a Sprint by manager fiat (only PO can)
- Sprint length > 1 month
- "Hardening" or "release" Sprint as a separate phase
