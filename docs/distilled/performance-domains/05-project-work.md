# Project Work — performance domain

**TL;DR:** Plan first, then work — but with a constant *two-way
dance* between plans and reality. Capture **follow-up items** as
they emerge; never let them slip.

## Plan-work balance

Three modes:
- **Working without a plan.** Team improvises against a vague
  mental image. Plan (if any) is updated *after* the fact, only
  one direction.
- **Working without flexibility.** Fixed detailed plan rigidly
  enforced. Unrealistic in any complex/complicated project.
- **Balanced (right).** Plans direct work; observed reality feeds
  back into plans. Continuous adjustment in both directions.

Concrete example: P3.express weekly kick-off — team reviews the
upcoming week to confirm the plan is still the best path and there
are no conflicts.

When plans change → measurements re-run → corrective or preventive
actions decided.

Strengthen the methodology in this area when projects are
high-uncertainty.

## Level of detail

- Management-layer plans should stop at *appropriate* detail; leave
  the rest to teams. Too much detail in management plans →
  inflexibility.
- Example layering (PRINCE2):
    - high-level plan upfront
    - stage plan immediately before each stage
    - **team plan** owned by the team, not management
- Trivial details belong in ad-hoc team decisions. Don't plan them.

## Follow-up items (P3.express term, broadly applicable)

Five things worth tracking in a single artifact (P3.express
*follow-up register*) — or in multiple artifacts in heavier methods:

1. **Risks** — uncertainties for the future.
   - Daily stand-up Q3 ("What problems *might* I have?") in Agile
     surfaces them. Adapt the cadence to project size.
2. **Issues** — unplanned things that already happened.
3. **Improvement plans** — outputs of satisfaction evaluations and
   retrospectives.
4. **Change requests** — assess impact (time/cost/quality/...),
   prepare proposals, requester picks. Change is fine *when
   managed*; the bug is unmanaged change.
5. **Lessons learned** — captured during the project, not just at
   close.

**Custodian rule (P3.express).** Every follow-up item *and* every
deliverable should have a named custodian who tracks it through to
closure.

Document items as soon as discovered — undocumented items consume
mental energy and risk being forgotten.

## When this domain matters more

- High-uncertainty projects
- Projects with frequent change requests or evolving scope
- Heavy ceremony loads → consolidate items into a follow-up register

## Anti-patterns

- Plan written once, work goes its own way
- Detailed management plan that the team can't deviate from
- Risks/issues/changes scattered across email and chat
- Items captured but no custodian, no follow-up
- Daily stand-up reduced to status-only with no risk-surfacing
