# Team — performance domain

**TL;DR:** Three concerns: team **structure**, team **building**,
and **leadership**. Two motivations: ethical (people deserve a safe
healthy environment regardless of project gain) and instrumental
(better team → better project).

## Structure of the team

- Methodologies provide a default structure. Adjust carefully —
  adding roles can break internal consistency.

### Centralized vs distributed PM
- **Centralized:** dedicated PM team (and a "project manager" on top).
- **Distributed:** PM activities spread across all team members; no
  dedicated PM.
- **Both valid.** Choice depends on project size and method.
  Distributed works only at small team sizes (cannot scale to ~100
  people without coordination).
- Many technical experts dislike PM responsibilities; forcing
  distribution onto them harms focus.
- *Project management always exists* even if a *project manager*
  role does not.

### PM in Agile projects
- DSDM and XP **have** a PM-equivalent role.
- Scrum **rejects** the PM role; bolting one on breaks Scrum's design.
- "Agile = no PM" is wrong — only true for Scrum and its derivatives.

### Sponsor (a.k.a. executive / business sponsor)
- A senior manager *above* the PM, providing:
    - resources via organizational power
    - high-level decisions aligned with org strategy
- Names: **sponsor** (P3.express, many), **executive** (PRINCE2),
  **business sponsor** (DSDM).

### Labels are unreliable
- Same job title means different things across orgs. Always check
  *responsibilities and authorities*, not the label.

## Team development

- Bake team-building into normal work (workshops, satisfaction
  reviews, retros) — outperforms standalone "team-building" events.
- Strengthen sense of mission. Help team see the meaning behind the
  work (e.g. "we're saving lives via this vaccine").
- Involve team in improvement plans → ownership.

## Leadership

Two questions for the PM:
1. How can I empower the **hidden leaders**?
2. How can I improve my own leadership skills?

Action: list leadership skills (motivation, problem solving,
conflict resolution, negotiation, facilitation, …); plan
self-development per skill; extend training to team leaders and
likely future PMs (give them facilitation duty + training).

**Win-win rule.** Pure win in negotiation is short-term; long-term
sustainability comes from win-win. Worth investing in negotiation
training.

## When this domain matters more

- Agile/Scrum rollout in a company that traditionally has PMs
- Programs with mixed centralized/distributed pockets
- Hiring/promoting new sponsors or PMs
- Talent retention conversations

## Anti-patterns

- Adding a "Project Manager" to Scrum
- Calling something "Agile = no PM" without checking the method
- Sponsor by title only; no real authority or strategy involvement
- Team building as standalone retreats disconnected from work
- PM hoarding leadership skills, no investment in team leaders
