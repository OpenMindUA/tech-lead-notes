# Foundational theory (SGE)

**TL;DR:** SGE explicitly grounds Scrum in **seven theoretical
foundations**: complexity, emergence, self-managing teams,
professionalism, lean thinking, empiricism, cadence. SG2020 only
named empiricism + lean.

## Complexity — the case for Scrum

- For complex work: **more unknowns than knowns**, expertise is
  necessary but **insufficient**, **cause and effect coherent only
  in retrospect**.
- Teams need time to **think, help each other, do rework, or pivot**.
- Cognitive diversity + empiricism handle complexity.
- Spaces shift over time (chaos → complex → ordered or reverse) —
  pause and verify which space you're actually in.
- "Product development often deals with unpredictability — Scrum is
  more useful than approaches with **delusions of predictability**."

## Emergence

- Patterns/behaviors arise from **interactions** in complex systems
  — **not** predictable from parts alone.
- Scrum doesn't tightly control emergence; it uses **enabling
  constraints** (timeboxes, roles, feedback loops) — "islands" in a
  sea of unpredictability.
- Influence rather than direct via short, parallel, **safe-to-fail
  experiments** + continuous feedback.
- Patterns: **Swarming, Stable Teams, Kaizen** identify and shape
  emergent behavior.
- "Self-management is **discovered** in the right environment, not
  designed top-down."
- Treats emergence as a **force to cultivate**, not a risk to eliminate.

## Self-managing Scrum Team

- Team checks if it's on track, takes action, decides how to work,
  resolves conflict, fixes problems internally.
- **Managers don't tell the team what to do.** If managers exist
  they should **demonstrate leadership** (not direction).
- Reliance on **non-self-managing teams** hinders complex work.
- Self-managing **team** ≠ individual self-management. The
  interplay among members is what matters.

## Professionalism

- **Excellence + accountability**, regardless of circumstances.
- "Cradle-to-grave" Product accountability: **maintenance** is
  included; ops gives engineering feedback.
- Software professionalism (non-exhaustive): Specification by
  Example, Clean Code, Unit Testing, **TDD**, Test Automation, **CI**,
  **CD**, Architecture & Design, Acceptance Testing, intentional
  testing.

## Lean thinking

- Reduce **waste**; focus on **flow of value** and **continuous
  improvement**.
- Built on **continuous improvement + respect for people**.
- Drives effectiveness at lowest long-term cost.

## Empiricism

- Decisions informed by **objective or observable evidence** in
  exploratory **learning cycles**.
- Cycle: observation → theory development/refinement →
  operationalization → testing/modification.
- **Evidence-informed** decision making is the response to
  experimentation.
- Pausing and reflecting combine empiricism + lean.
- "Inspecting and adapting should be **valued more** than keeping
  promises or serving the wrong stakeholders."
- "**All assumptions can be wrong.**"

## Cadence

- Sprint rhythm gives **clear short-term goals** + regular
  inspect-adapt.
- Builds **sustainable pace**, **predictability** and continuous
  improvement over time.

## When this applies

- Diagnosing whether Scrum is fit for the work (complexity check)
- Designing organizational change toward genuine self-management
- Coaching managers out of "tell-the-team" mode
- Defining technical-excellence expectations for SW Product Developers
- Setting an org-level definition of professionalism

## Anti-patterns

- Calling work "complex" when it's actually complicated → Scrum
  brings overhead without leverage
- Locking emergence out via heavy detailed plans
- "Self-managing" as code for **no management** at all (vs
  bounded autonomy)
- Professionalism reduced to compliance checklists
- Lean treated as cost-cutting only
